Human Random
Sequence Experiment
The Human Random Sequence Experiment is part of an exploration of the psychological and ethical issues we are confronted by due to our increasing dependence on technology. In particular, I'm interested in the effects of the pervasive integration of pattern recognition algorithms into our civilization.
We are usually unconscious of the presence of pattern recognition technology because it has been so subtly introduced into our daily lives. It is especially easy to ignore since it essentially mimics the way that humans think.
With the increasing application of pattern recognition algorithms to our behavior, such as we are seeing with companies like Google, Amazon, Target, Walmart, credit card companies, auto manufacturers, and of course our own government, questions of predictability and free will come into play.
The more behavioral information that is consolidated about you, the more accurate we can make predictions about your behavior. What does it mean to be spontaneous? Can a human really even be spontaneous?

Let's strip ourselves down to simplest terms: Let's just type 0s and 1s. OK, we can't actually be random, but what if we sequenced between people, where each person's nth input is added to the sequence in the nth round of selection? How many people does it take to create mathematical randomness? By crowdsourcing human attempts at random input, I'd like to show that it is through our interactions with others - society itself - that we generate the chaos of nature that is life itself.

A corporation may go to any length to get better intelligence about its customers. Here we have the most evasive input we can offer: an actual attempt to not provide something useful. How do we fare against the power of machine-driven pattern recognition?

The work itself requires participation by an audience. it becomes better more interesting as more people interact with it. it requires society.

The very act of attempting to be spontaneous or random, repeated over and over, person after person, as a sort of allegory for humans attempting to break out of the imprisonments of behavioral analysis by machines.

What kind if thing comes out of you when you attempt to be random? Is there any part of you there? If you try to break out of your own behavior, what are you? This exploration recalls that of the Dada, with their search for a paucity of meaning, and even further John Cage with his search for an absence of self.

Can we find that there is meaning in everything? Can there be a patternless space if our pattern recognition machines are trained sufficiently? Wouldn't the very creation of such a space (a patternless space) - or attempt to create it - immediately assign it meaning?
