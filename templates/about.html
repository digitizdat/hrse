Human Random
Sequence Experiment

This website is part of a larger project called Outsourcing Thought, which is an investigation of the psychological and ethical issues we are confronted by due to our increasing dependence on technology, and in particular this project focuses on pattern recognition algorithms.

We are usually unconscious of the presence of pattern recognition technology because has become so subtly and pervasively integrated into our daily lives. It is especially easy to ignore since it essentially mimics the way that humans think: by recognizing pattern after pattern in streams of input.

With the increasing application of pattern recognition algorithms to our behavior, such as we are seeing with companies like Google, Amazon, Target, Wallmart, credit card companies, auto manufacturers, and of course our own government, questions of predictability and free will come into play.

What does it mean to be spontaneous? Can a human being really be spontaneous?

Let's strip ourselves down to simplest terms: Let's just type 0s and 1s. OK, we can't be random, but what if we sequenced between people, where each person's nth input is added to the sequence in the nth round of selection? How many people does it take to create randomness?

Another interesting facet of this experiment is the search for relationships between participants. A corporation may go to any length to get better intelligence about its customers. Here we have the most seemingly benign input we can offer: an actual attempt to not provide something useful. How do we fare against the power of machine-driven pattern recognition?

The work itself requires participation by an audience. it becomes better more interesting as more people interact with it. it requires society.

The very act of attempting to be spontaneous or random, repeated over and over, person after person, as a sort of allegory for humans attempting to break out of the imprisonments of behavioral analysis by machines.

What kind if thing comes out of you when you attempt to be random? Is there any part of you there? If you try to break out of your own behavior, what are you? This exploration recalls that of the Dada, with their search for a paucity of meaning, and even further John Cage with his search for an absence of self.

Can we find that there is meaning in everything? Can there be a patternless space if our pattern recognition machines are trained sufficiently? Wouldn't the very creation of such a space (a patternless space) - or attempt to create it - immediately assign it meaning?
