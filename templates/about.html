Human Random
Sequence Experiment
"A child born today will grow up with no conception of privacy at all. They'll never know what it means to have a private moment to themselves: an unrecorded, unanalysed thought. And that's a problem because privacy matters. Privacy is what allows us to determine who we are and who we want to be." - Edward Snowden
We are breaking over a cultural threshold. Our struggle to cope with the growing volume of information we produce and collect has been relieved by our ability to project ourselves into our machines. Machine learning is a monumental achievement that enables us to extend our conscious minds to higher levels of global awareness without being overwhelmed. However, just as this technology expands us, it also creates new methods of control.
The Human Random Sequence Experiment is intended to evoke questions about the psychological and sociological effects of the integration of these algorithms into our daily lives, and in particular, questions about their use in behavior analysis and the dehumanization of people to data points.
Our behavior is subjected to analysis by algorithms with accelerating frequency by companies like Google, Amazon, Target, Walmart, credit card companies, auto manufacturers, and, of course, by our own government, which creates significant imbalances in our relationships with these entities. We are categorized in every conceivable way as our lives and our behavior patterns are digitized and archived. Questions about control, predictability, and free will come to mind: Can we control information about ourselves that is freely and involuntarily projected? What does it mean to be spontaneous? Can a person really even be spontaneous?
The more information about our behavior is consolidated, the more accurately a set of algorithms can make predictions about us. But it is important to keep in mind that it is we who are analyzing us. We make up the companies, we write the algorithms, we produce the data, and we store the data. We also create new knowledge in our analyses, which generates new data, and onward the cycle continues.
Let's strip ourselves down to simplest terms: Let's just type 0s and 1s. OK, we can't actually be mathematically random, but can we at least prevent our algorithms from recognizing who we are? What kind of thing comes out of you when you attempt to be random? Is there any part of you there? If you try to break out of your own behavior, what are you? These questions call to mind Marcel Duchamp's readymades, John Cage's quest for an absence of self, and Arman's Robot Portraits, which all seem ever more prescient since our technology has now surpassed our paranoid speculation.

What if we sequenced between people, where each person's nth input is added to the sequence in the nth round of selection? How many people does it take to create mathematical randomness? By crowdsourcing human attempts at random input, I'd like to show that it is through our interactions with others - society itself - that we generate the chaos of nature that is life itself.

A corporation may go to any length to get better intelligence about its customers. Here we have the most evasive, exiguous input we can offer: an actual attempt to not provide something useful. How do we fare against the power of machine-driven pattern recognition?

The work itself requires participation by an audience. The more participants provide sequences and accurate information for comparison, the interesting the exploration becomes.

The very act of attempting to be spontaneous or random, repeated over and over, person after person, as an allegory for the future: humans attempting to break out of the imprisonments of behavior analysis by machines, but in the process they themselves become machine-like.

Can we find that there is meaning in everything? Can there be a patternless space if our pattern recognition machines are trained sufficiently? Wouldn't the very act of creating such a space assign it meaning?
This website is part of a project by artist Martin McGreal called Outsourcing Thought, a study of the effects of the pervasive integration into society of machine-driven pattern recognition algorithms, and an exploration of the boundary between art and technology.
